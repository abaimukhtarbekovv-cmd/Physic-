4. Acceleration: Tangential and Normal Definition/Main Principle:



Tangential Acceleration (aₜ) — Component parallel to velocity; changes speed: aₜ = dv/dt.



Normal/Centripetal Acceleration (aₙ) — Component perpendicular to velocity, toward curvature center; changes direction: aₙ = v²/ρ (ρ = radius of curvature).



Total Acceleration (a) — Vector sum: a = aₜ + aₙ. Magnitude: a = √(aₜ² + aₙ²).



Physical Meaning & Explanation: Any curved or non-uniform motion decomposes acceleration this way. aₜ is due to change in speed; aₙ is due to change in direction. In uniform circular motion, aₜ=0 and aₙ is constant.



Relevant Formulas & Graphs: aₜ = d|v|/dt, aₙ = v²/ρ. For circular motion: ρ = r, aₙ = ω²r.



Graphs: For a car speeding up on a curve: aₜ(t) is constant positive, aₙ(t) increases (since v increases). The vector a points inward and forward.



Example: A planet in elliptical orbit: aₙ is largest at perihelion (smallest r), aₜ is non-zero except at peri/aphelion.
